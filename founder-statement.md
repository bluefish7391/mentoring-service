
# Informal version
I first thought of this program because I wanted to share the truly exciting parts of CS with younger students in a new, experimental way. When people think about CS, they usually imagine a programmer working on a website or maybe doing data analysis for some company. And then they see ChatGPT do exactly that in about 20 seconds. Admittedly, web design and full-stack development may be a little cooked. But there are so many crazy interesting CS topics beyond those that it would be criminal for an intelligent, analytical kid to never get a glimpse of them out of fear that the job market wouldn't support his passion. Imagine being insane at math your entire life, but the second a machine automates your homework, you give up on being a professor. For those young students who have yet to grasp how vast the realm of comp sci is, the chance to look at those wondrous topics could be the key to discovering a career they may have never considered. 

Why do you think it is that Hour of Code is popular? It's fun, accessible, and even better, it's easy to teach. Ridiculously easy. You don't even need an actual teacher. The skills taught by those programs also happen to be the ones most at risk of being taken over by AI, which gets to be more than a little discouraging. Don't get me wrong, every CS student should have basic programming skills. But by no means should it stop there. I am largely inspired by GHP, Flex Fridays at IA, and my own learning journey. When I was first thinking about this program, I set out to use the best parts of them. The way I see it, the best way that a lesson can benefit you is not by teaching you directly. Rather, the best lessons inspire you to Google one question on your own time. And the way we do that is with low-stakes, modular sessions. We can't teach quantum computing to a 5th grader for two hours a week. However, with some fun, engaging activities (and a lot of luck), we might show them that an operating system is something real and tangible. Out of curiosity, they stick around for a minute to ask a question after class because something clicked. Pretty soon, they’ve realized that there’s a whole nother level of computing that speaks to their love for creating powerful, beautifully designed tools. The key is that these sessions do have to be fun. As much as I would like to do a college style presentation or lecture, attention spans only last so long, and these sessions are voluntary. The number one priority for lessons is to be fun, only then can we be inspiring.

# Letter from the founder (to be refined at put on website)
"When most people think of computer science, they picture someone staring at a screen, building a website or analyzing a spreadsheet. With the rise of powerful AI tools, it’s easy for young students to wonder if that path is still worth pursuing.

But computer science is so much vaster than the code automated by a machine. I founded this program because it would be a tragedy for an analytical, creative kid to walk away from their passion out of fear of a changing job market.

Inspired by the best parts of my own learning journey, this program is built on a simple truth: lessons shouldn’t just teach; they should inspire. We don't lecture. We play, we simulate, and we explore. By showing middle schoolers that computer architecture and networks are tangible, exciting tools, we hope to give them a glimpse into a world of computing they never knew existed—and help them discover a future they might have never considered."
